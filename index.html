Unit I ‚Äî Introduction to Poetry (Core concepts)

What poetry does

Poetry condenses experience: it turns feeling, memory, thought, and image into a compact musical form. In the exam, focus on clear definitions and one strong example (line + small interpretation).

Key forms (short definitions you can write fast)

  • Epic ‚Äî Long narrative; public concerns; example: Homer‚Äôs Iliad.
  • Ballad ‚Äî Simple narrative, often with refrain.
  • Ode ‚Äî Formal lyric of praise (tone elevated).
  • Sonnet ‚Äî 14 lines; volta/turn often divides thought.
  • Elegy ‚Äî Meditation on loss; moves from lament to consolation.
  • Lyric ‚Äî Short, personal, emotional; ideal for close reading.

Poetic devices (write these as bullets in answers)

  • Metaphor, simile ‚Äî comparison without/with 'like'
  • Conceit ‚Äî extended intellectual metaphor (Donne)
  • Alliteration & assonance ‚Äî sound patterns
  • Blank verse ‚Äî unrhymed iambic pentameter (Milton)
  • Imagery ‚Äî sensory detail (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell)
Exam tip: For short notes write 2‚Äì3 lines definition + one quick example line. That gets full marks.

Homer ‚Äî Iliad (extract)

Shakespeare

  • Blow, Blow Thou Winter Wind ‚Äî world-weary tone, nature as mirror to human ingratitude.

John Milton ‚Äî excerpt

The extract emphasizes moral resolve and the sense of a divine/political order. Mention Milton‚Äôs use of blank verse and lofty diction.

Unit III ‚Äî Neo-Classical Poetry

Alexander Pope

Pope balances order and moral reflection. In answers mention his use of heroic couplets and satirical sharpness. Quick idea: in An Essay on Man he argues for a limited human perspective ‚Äî ‚ÄúWhatever is, is right‚Äù (note the debate around this line).

Thomas Gray ‚Äî Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard

Key themes for exam: mortality, the worth of common lives, memory and epitaphic tone. Learn 2‚Äì3 lines (opening & closing) to anchor your answer.

Exam tip: Gray often appears in long answers ‚Äî build answers around theme, tone, and social message.

Section A ‚Äî Exact answers (copy them, learn them)

Memorize any five. Each block is 50‚Äì70 words and exam-ready.

1) Poetry is a literary form that expresses emotions and ideas through rhythm, imagery, and symbolic language. It differs from prose in its musical structure and condensed expression. 2) A sonnet is a fourteen-line poem written in iambic pentameter, commonly associated with themes of love, time, and beauty. The sonnet often contains a turn (volta). 3) An elegy is a poem of mourning that reflects on death, loss, and the passage of time; it commonly moves from lament towards consolation or reflection. 4) Metaphysical poetry is characterised by intellectual imagery, philosophical themes, and extended conceits ‚Äî its rhetoric is argumentative and playful, as in Donne. 5) Romantic poetry emphasises emotion, imagination, nature, and individual experience, reacting against neoclassical restraint and valuing spontaneity and insight.

Section B ‚Äî How to write a 15-mark answer

  1. Intro (1‚Äì2 sentences): Name the poem, poet, context (age/genre).
  2. Summary (2‚Äì3 lines): Give a clear, concise overview of the poem‚Äôs action or argument.
  3. Themes (3‚Äì4 points): Bulleted ‚Äî list central themes with one short explanation each.
  4. Imagery & Language (2‚Äì3 bullets): Mention key devices and a short quote (1 line).
  5. Tone & Structure: Note voice, stanza form, and any important shifts (volta/turn).
  6. Conclusion (1‚Äì2 lines): Sum up the poem‚Äôs significance or poet‚Äôs aim.

Write legibly, use headings, and include one short quoted line per answer ‚Äî it shows confidence with the text.

  2. Summary: The speaker reflects in a rural churchyard, thinking of lives unrecorded by fame yet worthy of dignity.

  4. Imagery & language: pastoral imagery, epitaphic tone; sample line: ‚ÄúThe paths of glory lead but to the grave.‚Äù
  5. Conclusion: Gray argues for humble virtue‚Äôs dignity and warns against arrogance of fame.

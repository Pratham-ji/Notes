Unit I ‚Äî Introduction to Poetry (Core concepts) ‚Äî Detailed

1. What poetry does ‚Äî expanded

Poetry compresses thought and feeling. Where prose explains, poetry suggests: it leaves spaces that a reader must inhabit. For exam answers, say this plainly and illustrate with one example (e.g., ‚ÄúDaffodils uses memory to transform a simple scene into moral calm‚Äù).

2. Forms ‚Äî with quick exam cues

  • Epic ‚Äî long narrative, heroic action, public values. (Cue: mention plot & heroic code.)
  • Ballad ‚Äî narrative + refrain, often oral origins. (Cue: rhythm and repetition.)
  • Ode ‚Äî elevated praise, ceremonial tone. (Cue: address/ apostrophe.)
  • Sonnet ‚Äî compact argument + volta/turn (Shakespearean: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG). Always point out the turn when asked.
  • Elegy ‚Äî lament + consolation (structure: lament ‚Üí praise ‚Üí consolation).
  • Lyric ‚Äî personal feeling; best for close reading and language points.

3. Devices ‚Äî what to spot and how to write about them

When a poem asks for ‚Äúlanguage‚Äù or ‚Äústyle‚Äù, use this checklist and quote a short phrase (1‚Äì6 words) as evidence:

  • Sound: alliteration / assonance / rhyme ‚Äî mention how sound matches meaning (e.g., soft sounds for calm).
  • Imagery: visual, auditory, tactile ‚Äî link an image to emotion.
  • Figurative speech: metaphor, simile, personification ‚Äî explain briefly what two things the poet compares and why.
  • Form & structure: stanza length, rhyme scheme, enjambment ‚Äî say what the structure does (e.g., breaks thought, speeds up pace).
  • Tone & voice: speaker‚Äôs attitude ‚Äî isolate words that show irony, wonder, anger, tenderness.

4. Quick one-paragraph sample answer (use as template)

Answer (sample, 6‚Äì8 lines): Wordsworth‚Äôs Daffodils suggests that memory is a moral resource. The poem transforms a visual scene of flowers into a lasting interior joy; images of ‚Äúdancing‚Äù daffodils and the solitude of the speaker create contrast. The final stanza shows that recollection restores tranquility ‚Äî memory is the mind‚Äôs garden that can be tended and returned to in difficulty. (Finish by naming poetic device: personification and recollection.)

5. Probable short questions (Section A style)

  • Define lyric poem with an example.
  • Explain blank verse and give a line feature.
  • What is a conceit? Give a short example (Donne).
Exam tip: For any short note, begin with a one-line definition, then add one example line and one effect sentence. That wins full marks.

Unit II ‚Äî Classical & Metaphysical Poetry ‚Äî Detailed notes & models

Homer ‚Äî short context + what to write (extract from Iliad)

Context: The Iliad centers on the Trojan War and the tension between personal honour and communal fate. For an extract, identify the speaker and situation (who is speaking? what triggered the scene?).

How to answer: Summarize the extract (2 lines), mention its thematic link to the epic (e.g., heroism, mortality), and point to Homer's style (epic similes, elevated diction).

Shakespeare ‚Äî songs & Sonnet 30

  • ‚ÄúBlow, Blow, Thou Winter Wind‚Äù ‚Äî Tone: bitterly comic; technique: apostrophe to the wind, antithesis (wind vs human ingratitude). Exam cue: say how nature exposes human ingratitude.
  • Sonnet 30 ‚Äî Theme: recollection & consolation; structure: problem (loss) ‚Üí recovery (memory of friend). Cue: point to volta where consolation arrives.

John Donne ‚Äî A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning

Context & purpose: A farewell poem where the speaker reassures his beloved of spiritual unity despite physical separation.

Key features to write about:

  • Conceit of the compass: the two lovers like the two legs ‚Äî they remain one movement. Explain how the conceit fuses idea and feeling.
  • Tone: calm, confident, philosophical. Point to the paradox that separation deepens love‚Äôs ‚Äúsublimity‚Äù.
  • Form: metaphysical wit ‚Äî abrupt images that force intellectual engagement.

Short model paragraph (exam): Donne‚Äôs compass conceit connects physical geometry to emotional fidelity; the sturdiness of the compass leg images the constancy and guidance of love. The effect is to elevate domestic devotion to metaphysical truth.

John Milton ‚Äî extract (style points)

  • Write about Milton‚Äôs blank verse: formal, elevated diction, syntactic complexity.
  • Note rhetorical devices: apostrophe to God or fate, use of antithesis (earthly/political tensions).
  • Exam cue: point out one or two strong images and explain their effect.

Probable long-answer (example)

Q: ‚ÄúExplain the use of conceits in Donne and how they shape meaning.‚Äù

A: Define conceit, give the compass example and one additional (e.g., beaten gold or the cathedral image), and explain how the intellectual surprise makes the feeling clearer ‚Äî it demands reader reasoning ‚Äî end with a sentence about tone and audience.

Unit III ‚Äî Neo-Classical Poetry ‚Äî Detailed

Alexander Pope ‚Äî short guide

Context: Pope writes in the Augustan tradition: balance, order, satiric voice. Use heroic couplets (two-line rhymed units) to discuss form and its epigrammatic effect.

What to write: Identify Pope‚Äôs moral argument (e.g., limits of human reason in An Essay on Man), show one couplet‚Äôs function, and mention satire as social critique.

Exam line to memorize: Don‚Äôt quote long lines ‚Äî refer to the idea: ‚ÄúPope compresses moral paradox into neat couplets‚Äù and give an example paraphrase.

Thomas Gray ‚Äî Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard

Context: Mid-18th-century reflection on ordinary lives. The elegy is not just private grief; it is public moral instruction.

Structure & how to write it in exam

  • Opening lines: set the setting (churchyard at evening) ‚Äî note mood & landscape.
  • Middle stanzas: recall the common man‚Äôs unheralded worth ‚Äî use examples: husbandry, simple virtue.
  • Final stanzas: consolatory, general moral: fame is fragile; virtue is true value.

Key things to quote/paraphrase

Use short paraphrases like ‚Äúthe paths of glory lead to the grave‚Äù (paraphrase the theme instead of quoting long lines). Identify imagery: ‚Äúrural stillness‚Äù, ‚Äúepitaphic voice‚Äù.

Sample 10-line model answer (Gray)

Gray‚Äôs elegy solemnly records the dignity of ordinary lives. The rural imagery creates a reflective mood; the poem‚Äôs turn to memory and epitaph shows that social worth is not only public renown but private virtue. In examinations, emphasize the egalitarian theme and Gray‚Äôs use of pastoral devices to dignify everyday labour.

Unit IV ‚Äî Romantic Poetry ‚Äî Detailed

William Blake ‚Äî The Tyger (how to answer)

Core idea: Blake explores creation‚Äôs paradox ‚Äî fierce beauty & fearful symmetry. He asks who could craft both the lamb and the tiger.

  • Imagery: fire & forge; blacksmith imagery (creation as craft).
  • Form: regular trochaic rhythm ‚Äî creates a hammering effect (imitates forging).
  • Exam cue: mention innocence/experience contrast (Blake‚Äôs two books).

Wordsworth ‚Äî I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud (Daffodils)

What to emphasise: Wordsworth‚Äôs use of nature as restorative memory. The poem progresses from solitary feeling to the recollected bliss that sustains the speaker later.

  • Point out device: personification of daffodils (they ‚Äúdance‚Äù).
  • Note theme: imagination & recollection ‚Äî memory converts passing sight into moral calm.

Coleridge ‚Äî Dejection: An Ode

Focus on the speaker‚Äôs failure of imaginative power and the resulting melancholic tone; discuss how the poem's shift from outward description to inward confession maps the poet's crisis.

Shelley ‚Äî To a Skylark

The skylark is an emblem of spontaneous joy; Shelley uses ecstatic imagery and hypothetical comparisons (poetry can‚Äôt fully capture the skylark‚Äôs felicity). Mention Shelley‚Äôs idealism and musical syntax.

Keats ‚Äî Ode to a Nightingale

Stress Keats‚Äôs sensual images and the tension between transience (mortality) and the nightingale‚Äôs seeming eternity. Discuss the poem‚Äôs desire to escape through imagination and the final acceptance of mortality.

Quick exam strategy (Romantics)

  • Always quote one short line (under 15 words) as a textual anchor.
  • Note the poem‚Äôs emotional movement (beginning ‚Üí middle ‚Üí end): describe how language changes to match feeling.

Unit V ‚Äî Victorian & Modern Poetry ‚Äî Detailed

Tennyson ‚Äî Break, Break, Break

Speak about the poem‚Äôs use of the sea as rhythmic background to personal grief; structure is short and brittle, echoing the speaker‚Äôs broken feeling. Mention use of repetition & short clauses to mimic waves and interruption.

Robert Browning ‚Äî Prospice

Browning confronts death with defiance; the poem is dramatic and celebrates courage (‚ÄúI shall be more‚Äù ‚Äî paraphrase). Analyze the speaker‚Äôs voice and use of heroic tone.

W. B. Yeats ‚Äî A Prayer for My Daughter

Yeats blends public anxiety and private wish: his prayer is for stability, rootedness, moral order for his child. Use the poem‚Äôs political context (post-revolution) to explain the anxious mood.

Sarojini Naidu ‚Äî Indian Weavers

Focus on imagery of craft; the poem celebrates communal work, tradition, and the music of labour. Use cultural vocabulary as answer points ‚Äî colour, rhythm, tactile images.

Eunice de Souza ‚Äî Marriages Are Made

This modern poem is sharp and ironic. Point out its conversational diction, its domestic images, and the way irony reveals social constraints.

Exam quick checks (modern poems)

  • Identify tone (ironic, bitter, elegiac). Modern poems often reward a statement about social context.
  • Short quotes + a note on diction (colloquial vs elevated) win marks.

Section A ‚Äî Exact answers (copy them, learn them)

Memorize any five. Each block is 50‚Äì70 words and exam-ready.

1) Poetry is a literary form that expresses emotions and ideas through rhythm, imagery, and symbolic language. It differs from prose in its musical structure and condensed expression. 2) A sonnet is a fourteen-line poem written in iambic pentameter, commonly associated with themes of love, time, and beauty. Section B ‚Äî How to write a 15-mark answer (Detailed, spoon-fed)

Follow this structured template. Write headings in the answer, keep neat paragraphs, and include one short textual quote (1‚Äì10 words) in every answer.

  1. Intro (2 lines): State poet, poem, period; one-line thesis (what the poem does).
  2. Summary (2‚Äì3 lines): Very concise ‚Äî avoid plot detail except where relevant.
  3. Themes (3 bullets): Each theme ‚Äî 1 sentence example + short explanation.
  4. Imagery & devices (3 bullets): Name device, quote 1‚Äì8 words, say effect.
  5. Tone & structure: Note stanza form, any shifts (volta), sound patterns.
  6. Conclusion (2 lines): Sum up poet‚Äôs aim and poem‚Äôs lasting effect.

Model answer 1 ‚Äî Donne: A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning (handwriting-ready)

  1. Intro: John Donne‚Äôs lyric reassures a beloved about separation ‚Äî metaphysical conceits replace emotional panic with reasoned faith.
  2. Summary: The speaker instructs against public displays of grief and uses the compass conceit to show spiritual unity despite distance.
  3. Themes:
    • Enduring love ‚Äî beyond physical separation.
    • Spiritual unity ‚Äî love as metaphysical bond.
    • Calm stoicism ‚Äî the proper response to parting.
  4. Imagery & devices:
    • Conceit (compass): maps emotion to geometry.
    • Paradox: ‚Äútwo, they are one‚Äù ‚Äî unity in separation.
  5. Tone & structure: Argumentative, calm; use of irregular metre heightens rhetorical control.
  6. Conclusion: Donne transforms personal farewell into philosophical proof of love‚Äôs endurance.

Model answer 2 ‚Äî Wordsworth: Daffodils

  1. Intro: Wordsworth‚Äôs lyric celebrates the mind‚Äôs power to transform a visual scene into ongoing joy.
  2. Summary: A speaker sees dancing daffodils and later retrieves the image as comforting memory.
  3. Themes: Nature as solace; memory & imagination; solitude to connectedness.
  4. Imagery & devices: Personification (flowers ‚Äúdance‚Äù); visual images; simple diction for accessibility.
  5. Tone & structure: Reflective; four stanzas moving from sight to recollection.
  6. Conclusion: The poem argues that memory is a moral resource that sustains inner peace.

Model answer 3 ‚Äî Gray: Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard

  1. Intro: Gray‚Äôs elegy reflects on the dignity of ordinary lives and the limits of fame.
  2. Summary: In a rural setting the speaker imagines the lives of the dead and recommends humility.
  3. Themes: Mortality; equality of death; memory‚Äôs moral function.
  4. Imagery & devices: Pastoral imagery; epitaphic tone; rhetorical questions to universalize experience.
  5. Conclusion: Gray dignifies humble virtue and cautions the proud about transience.
